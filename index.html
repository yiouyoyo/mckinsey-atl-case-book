Interview Questions
Q1
Framework / Structure
Before we look at the data, how would you structure your approach to diagnosing the ER throughput crisis and identifying where the biggest value levers are?
Model Answer

I'd structure this across three MECE buckets: demand drivers, process/operational efficiency, and financial impact. Within each:

  • Demand: Is volume growth outpacing capacity? What's the acuity mix — are ESI 4/5 (low-acuity) patients clogging the ER who could be redirected to urgent care?
  • Process: Where in the patient journey is time being lost? The key stages are: triage → bed assignment → physician → treatment/diagnostics → discharge. I'd benchmark each stage and identify the longest relative bottleneck.
  • Capacity: Beds, physician hours, nursing ratios, ancillary support (lab/imaging turnaround) — is the constraint physical, human, or workflow-based?
  • Financial impact: Three levers — CMS VBP penalty ($3.8M), LWBS revenue leakage, and boarding costs from inpatient capacity pressure.

My hypothesis going in: given the LWBS rate is 4× benchmark, I suspect the main bottleneck is bed-to-physician time driven by a staffing mismatch or boarding problem, not triage speed per se.

Q2
Exhibit Interpretation
Look at Exhibit 1. What does the data tell you? Where is the biggest operational problem, and what does it suggest about root cause?
Model Answer

Three observations stand out:

  • Bed assignment is the primary bottleneck: 58 minutes vs. a 20-minute benchmark — a 38-minute gap. This is the single largest absolute and relative deviation. It points to a bed management problem: either boarding patients are occupying ED beds while awaiting inpatient placement, or there's no real-time bed coordination system.
  • Physician-to-treatment is also elevated: 72 minutes vs. 45 — suggests physician coverage is stretched, likely due to high ESI 4/5 (non-urgent) patient volume diluting physician availability for higher-acuity patients.
  • Triage and discharge are close to benchmark: These stages are functioning reasonably well. Investing there would yield limited return.

The boarding hypothesis is supported: if admitted patients are occupying ED beds while waiting for inpatient rooms, bed assignment time balloons and ripples through the entire flow. Root fix likely requires inpatient bed management improvements (discharge planning, throughput coordinators) not just ER-side interventions.

Q3
Math / Quantitative
The hospital is considering hiring 6 additional ER nurses at a loaded cost of $115,000 each. If this reduces LWBS from 8.2% to 4.0%, is the investment justified on a purely financial basis? Show your work.
Model Answer — Worked Calculation
Step 1 — Annual cost of 6 nurses: 6 nurses × $115,000 = $690,000/year Step 2 — LWBS patients recovered: Current LWBS: 8.2% × 68,400 = 5,609 patients Target LWBS: 4.0% × 68,400 = 2,736 patients Recovered patients: 5,609 − 2,736 = 2,873 patients/year Step 3 — Revenue per recovered patient: Blended avg ER revenue = $1,416/visit (from Exhibit 2) Note: LWBS skews toward Medicaid/self-pay, so apply discount Conservative estimate: $1,200/recovered visit Step 4 — Revenue recovered: 2,873 × $1,200 = $3.45M/year Step 5 — Net benefit: $3.45M − $0.69M = $2.76M net gain Step 6 — CMS VBP: partial recovery if LWBS metric improves If LWBS falls below 5% threshold, estimate 50% penalty recovery 0.50 × $3.8M = $1.9M additional recovery Combined upside: $2.76M + $1.9M = $4.66M vs. $690K investment ROI: 575%; Payback: ~7 weeks

Conclusion: Yes, the investment is strongly justified. Even without CMS benefit, the LWBS recovery alone returns 5× the cost. The bigger constraint is likely whether 6 nurses alone can drive a 420bps LWBS reduction — the bed management root cause also needs to be addressed for sustainable improvement.

Q4
Recommendation + Curveball
Give me your final recommendation to the CEO. Then: the CFO pushes back — "We've tried staffing increases before and LWBS didn't improve. Why will this time be different?"
Model Answer

Recommendation: Greystone should pursue a two-track intervention. In the short term (0–3 months): implement real-time bed management with a dedicated throughput coordinator role, redirect ESI 4/5 patients to an attached fast-track urgent care pathway, and add nursing coverage during peak hours (10am–10pm). In the medium term (3–12 months): work with inpatient nursing and case management to reduce boarding through earlier discharge rounds and bed notification protocols. This combination can realistically reduce LOS from 4.2 to 3.3 hours and LWBS from 8.2% to below 3%, recovering the CMS penalty and $3–4M in LWBS leakage.

Interviewer Pushback

"We've added nurses before and it didn't move the needle. Why is this different?"

Response to pushback: That's a fair challenge and actually validates a key point in my analysis. If prior staffing increases didn't help, it confirms the constraint isn't nursing headcount alone — it's the boarding problem. When admitted patients occupy ED beds waiting for inpatient rooms, adding ER nurses doesn't free up beds. The fix requires inpatient-side action: hardwiring 9am discharge rounds, holding 5% of inpatient beds as "swing" for ED boarders, and empowering a real-time throughput coordinator with authority to escalate. Nursing investment only works when paired with the bed availability fix upstream.

Interview Questions
Q1
Framework / Structure
How would you approach diagnosing a 7-point EBIT margin decline for a retailer with flat revenues? Walk me through your structure.
Model Answer

EBIT = Revenue − COGS − Operating Costs. Since revenue is roughly flat, I'd focus the diagnostic on the cost side, but in two phases:

  • Phase 1 — P&L decomposition: Waterfall from prior EBIT to current EBIT, separating revenue mix effects, gross margin compression, and opex changes. I'd look at COGS, SG&A, and D&A separately to isolate which bucket is responsible.
  • Phase 2 — Root cause by cost bucket:
    • COGS: Inflation in input costs, freight, ocean shipping? Private label vs. national brand mix shift? Markdown/inventory issues?
    • SG&A: Labor inflation (minimum wage increases)? New store ramp costs? Corporate overhead creep?
    • Revenue: Why is comp-store sales negative despite flat total revenue? Suggests new store growth masking weakness.

My going-in hypothesis: given the macro environment, I'd suspect supply chain COGS inflation is the primary driver (external), with negative comp-store sales adding revenue mix pressure (partially internal/competitive).

Q2
Exhibit Interpretation
Look at Exhibits 1 and 2. What are your three most important takeaways, and what actions do they suggest?
Model Answer
  • Takeaway 1 — Supply chain is the dominant culprit: The waterfall shows $28M of the $33.4M EBIT decline comes from COGS inflation, with ocean freight (+185% YoY) as the single biggest item at $14.2M. This is partly external/macro, but there's likely a vendor contract management opportunity — are freight contracts locked in at peak rates? Is there a near-shore sourcing option?
  • Takeaway 2 — Bottom 50 stores are structurally unprofitable: Tier 3 stores show −6.9% contribution margin. Even if supply chain normalizes, these stores lose ~$96K each, totaling ~$4.8M in drag. The CEO should immediately evaluate closure/renegotiation for the worst performers — break-even on closure requires only that lease exit cost < NPV of ongoing losses.
  • Takeaway 3 — SG&A deleverage is a secondary issue: SG&A increased $7M on essentially flat revenue, suggesting the company opened stores and added corporate staff assuming revenue growth that didn't materialize. The new stores are likely Tier 3-level performers causing the comp drag.

Priority actions: (1) Renegotiate freight contracts or diversify to near-shore sourcing; (2) Closure/lease renegotiation for bottom 30–50 stores; (3) Freeze new store openings until comp productivity recovers.

Q3
Math / Quantitative
The team is evaluating closing the bottom 50 Tier 3 stores. Average lease break costs are $180K per store. If store contribution is −$96K annually and cost of capital is 10%, what is the NPV of closing all 50? Should the CEO do it?
Model Answer — Worked Calculation
Step 1 — Annual loss being avoided per store: $96K contribution loss × 50 stores = $4.8M/year saved Step 2 — Lease exit cost (one-time): $180K × 50 stores = $9.0M upfront cost Step 3 — Assume remaining lease life = 3 years average Annual benefit: $4.8M/year PV of benefit stream (10% cost of capital, 3 years): PV = $4.8M × [(1 − 1/1.1³) / 0.10] = $4.8M × 2.487 = $11.94M Step 4 — NPV of closure program: NPV = PV of savings − Upfront lease costs NPV = $11.94M − $9.0M = +$2.94M Step 5 — Sensitivity: What if lease life is only 2 years? PV = $4.8M × 1.736 = $8.33M NPV = $8.33M − $9.0M = −$0.67M (barely negative) Conclusion: At 3+ years remaining lease life, closure is NPV-positive. For stores with < 2 years remaining, wait for natural lease expiry.

Recommendation: Close stores with ≥ 3 years remaining on leases immediately. Allow shorter-term leases to expire naturally. The CFO should also negotiate lease modifications (rent concessions) for mid-tier stores before resorting to exits.

Q4
Recommendation + Curveball
Synthesize your recommendation to the CEO. Then: she pushes back — "If we close 50 stores, won't we lose the purchasing scale that keeps our COGS competitive with bigger chains?"
Model Answer

Recommendation: Southern Living Goods faces a two-part crisis: an external supply chain shock worth $28M and a structural portfolio problem with 50 loss-making stores. I recommend three actions: First, immediately pursue freight contract renegotiation and accelerate near-shore sourcing pilots to offset $8–12M of COGS inflation over 18 months. Second, close or renegotiate leases on the bottom 30–50 stores with long remaining lease lives, recovering $2–5M NPV. Third, freeze new store openings and redirect capital to private label expansion in Tier 1/2 stores, where gross margin is 8pp higher than branded. Together these actions should restore EBIT margins to 8–9% within 24 months.

Interviewer Pushback

"Won't closing 50 stores destroy our purchasing scale and hurt COGS even more?"

Response: That's a legitimate concern worth quantifying. The 50 Tier 3 stores represent roughly 27% of store count but only ~15% of revenue (given their $1.4M avg vs. $2.6M chain avg). Most large vendors price by annual dollar volume, not store count. A more precise answer requires reviewing the top 10 vendor contracts — if volume discounts are structured by store count, we may need to renegotiate terms. But in most retail purchasing agreements I've seen, total dollar volume is the dominant lever. Closing $70M in low-productivity revenue is a manageable negotiating adjustment compared to the ongoing $4.8M annual drain.

Framework / Structure
How would you structure the evaluation of whether Peach State should acquire MedRoute at $132 million?
Model Answer

I'd evaluate this acquisition across four areas:

  • 1. Strategic rationale: Does MedRoute fill a capability gap or expand into an attractive adjacency? Medical supply delivery is structurally different from general freight — it requires temperature-controlled vehicles, HIPAA compliance, and hospital-grade reliability. Is Peach State capable of operating and growing this business? Is this market growing (outpatient shift, aging population)? Does the geography overlap create route density?
  • 2. Financial returns: Is 8.5× a fair price? What do comparable medical logistics transactions look like? (Sector comps: 7–10× for specialized medical logistics.) What IRR does the PE sponsor need — typically 20–25% for mid-market PE — and what revenue growth and margin assumptions are required to hit that?
  • 3. Risk assessment: Customer concentration is the headline risk — 68% from 3 customers, two of whose contracts expire within 18 months of close. Also regulatory (HIPAA, DEA for controlled substance delivery), integration risk (driver culture, dispatch systems), and competitive moat durability.
  • 4. Alternatives: Organic build vs. acquisition. Can Peach State enter medical delivery organically? What's the time and cost? Are there other acquisition targets that trade at lower multiples or have less concentration risk?

Bottom line question: Is $132M a price that provides sufficient upside given the customer concentration tail risk?

Q2
Exhibit Interpretation
What do the two exhibits tell you about the attractiveness and risks of this deal?
Model Answer
  • Exhibit 1 — The margin story is compelling: MedRoute's 18% EBITDA margin versus Peach State's 9% reflects the premium economics of specialized medical logistics. If Peach State can protect those margins post-acquisition, the combined entity will have meaningfully higher margin quality than Peach State standalone. The question is whether margins are defensible or depend on the concentrated customer relationships.
  • Exhibit 2 — Customer concentration is existential, not just a footnote: Piedmont (32.6% of revenue) has a contract expiring in December 2025 — likely before or shortly after deal close. If Piedmont chooses to re-tender or switch to a national provider, the acquisition economics collapse. At $28M revenue × 18% margin = $5.0M EBITDA from Piedmont alone. Losing Piedmont post-close effectively destroys 32% of the deal's EBITDA base. This should be a hard deal condition: contract extensions or LOIs from top 2 customers before signing.
  • Synergies are credible but not transformative: $3.8M is reasonable and well-sourced (route, back-office, procurement). They improve the entry multiple from 8.5× to 6.8× post-synergy, which is at the low end of logistics M&A comps — attractive if you believe the customer base is stable.

Math / Quantitative
The PE sponsor needs a 22% IRR on a 5-year hold. Assuming $132M purchase price, $15M of acquisition debt at 7% interest, and a 7.5× exit multiple, what EBITDA does the combined company need to generate in Year 5? Is it achievable?

Step 1 — Equity invested: Purchase price: $132M Less acquisition debt: $15M (assume from deal structure) Equity check: $117M Step 2 — Required equity value at exit (22% IRR, 5 years): Future Value = $117M × (1.22)^5 (1.22)^5 = 1.22 × 1.22 × 1.22 × 1.22 × 1.22 ≈ 1.488 × 1.488 × 1.22 ≈ 2.703 Target equity value at exit ≈ $117M × 2.703 = $316.3M Step 3 — Debt at exit (assume debt paid down to $10M): Exit enterprise value = Equity + Net Debt = $316.3M + $10M = $326.3M Step 4 — Required Year 5 EBITDA at 7.5× exit: EBITDA = $326.3M / 7.5× = $43.5M Step 5 — Required EBITDA growth: Day 1 combined EBITDA: $28.8M + $15.5M + $3.8M synergies = $48.1M Wait — combined EBITDA exceeds required exit EBITDA? Recalculate: Day 1 EBITDA is already $48.1M (including synergies). Required: $43.5M (slightly less than today!) Implication: At current synergy-adjusted EBITDA, the returns work even with zero organic growth — as long as the exit multiple holds. The deal is "multiple compression proof" if EBITDA is maintained. Sensitivity: If Piedmont contract is lost: Piedmont EBITDA loss: $28M × 18% = $5.0M Adjusted Day 1 EBITDA: $48.1M − $5.0M = $43.1M Required 5yr EBITDA for target return: $43.5M Required growth: $0.4M on $43.1M base = ~1% CAGR — barely achievable

Conclusion: The returns math works at current EBITDA levels, but the Piedmont contract loss would push the deal to the margin of viability. The PE firm should make contract lock-up a hard condition of close, or negotiate a 10–15% price reduction to buffer customer risk.

If we underwrite 15% growth and the pharma expansion doesn't materialize, we've overpaid.

Before looking at the data, how would you structure the question of whether First Georgia should enter SMB digital lending, and the build vs. partner sub-question?
Model Answer

I'd structure this as two sequential questions: first, should we enter at all? Then, how should we enter?

  • Should we enter?

Q2
Exhibit Interpretation
What does the data tell you? Which path — build or partner — would you lean toward, and why?
Model Answer
  • The 5-year NPV favors Build by $4.3M ($28.4M vs. $24.1M), but the gap is narrower than many assume because of the 18–24 month time-to-market delay. Build foregoes ~$6M in NII during the ramp period that Partner would capture — and the NPV analysis likely assumes some growth during that period for Build.
  • Unit economics strongly favor Build for scale: Build LTV of $6,740 vs. Partner LTV of $3,872 — Build is 74% more valuable per loan because there's no 30% revenue share. At 3,000+ loans, that's a $8.6M annual NII difference.
  • The regulatory and data angle tips the decision: For a regulated bank, maintaining full customer data ownership and regulatory control is strategically valuable beyond the financial comparison. Regulators (OCC, FDIC) are scrutinizing bank-fintech partnerships closely; compliance risk from a shared arrangement can be hard to manage.

My lean: Partner first, build in parallel. Use a fintech partner to enter the market in 6 months and start building the loan book and customer data. Simultaneously invest in building proprietary capabilities over 18–24 months. Transition off the partnership as build capability matures. This hybrid approach captures time-to-market benefits while preserving long-term economics and capability ownership.

Probably ambitious but achievable given: · First Georgia has existing SMB relationships (not starting cold) · Digital platform reaches clients nationally, not just local · However, fintech incumbents already have traction Revised realistic target: 12–15% share = $10–12M NII in Year 5

Implication: The $20M NII target is aggressive. The bank should set a more realistic Year 5 target of $10–12M in digital SMB NII, building to $20M+ by Year 7–8 as the loan book seasons and market share grows. This affects the NPV calculation — both paths are still financially positive, but returns are back-end weighted.

Recommendation + Curveball
Give your recommendation to the CEO. Then: the board pushes back — "Our Chief Credit Officer says digital SMB lending has 3–5× higher default rates than branch-originated loans. Doesn't that kill the economics?"
Model Answer

Recommendation: First Georgia should enter SMB digital lending via a phased hybrid approach: (1) Sign a fintech partnership within 90 days to enter the market rapidly, target 500 loans in Year 1 to build data and brand; (2) simultaneously invest $14M over 18 months to build proprietary digital underwriting — using Year 1–2 fintech loan data to train credit models with local SMB behavior; (3) transition to the owned platform in Year 3, exiting the revenue-share arrangement. This approach captures the $24M NPV of partnership economics in Years 1–2 while building toward the $28M+ NPV of ownership in Years 3–5. The board should authorize an initial $2M for the fintech partnership evaluation and technology roadmap immediately.

Board / CCO Pushback

"Digital SMB lending defaults are 3–5× higher than our branch loans. How do the economics work?"

Response: That's a critical point worth examining carefully. Our unit economics already assume a 2.8% loss rate — about 2.5× our current branch SMB default rate of ~1.1%. If the CCO is right that defaults could be 3–5×, let's stress test: at 5× = 5.5% default rate, annual credit cost per loan becomes $10,175. Against $8,880 NII and $2,664 revenue share (if partnered), the economics flip negative. So the key question is not whether defaults are higher — they are — but whether our underwriting model can segment risk well enough to keep portfolio defaults below 3%. Modern digital underwriting using alternative data (cash flow, accounts receivable, real-time bank feed) has meaningfully improved SMB credit prediction versus traditional FICO-based models. I'd recommend a 6-month pilot of 300–400 loans with the fintech partner to empirically validate our loss rates before committing to full-scale build. If losses exceed 3.5%, we pause the build decision.
